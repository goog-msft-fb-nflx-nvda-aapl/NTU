01

Homography Estimation & Forward Warping

A homography is a projective transformation between two planes, represented as a 3×3 matrix H. It maps any point on one plane to its corresponding point on another, preserving straight lines but not parallelism or distances.

Given at least four point correspondences, the homography is estimated using the Direct Linear Transform (DLT) method. Each correspondence contributes two linear equations, forming an overdetermined system Ah = 0. The solution is the eigenvector of ATA corresponding to the smallest eigenvalue, found via SVD — specifically, the last row of VT.

With the homography in hand, each source image is forward-warped onto the destination canvas: every source pixel coordinate is projected through H to its destination location, where it is deposited via nearest-neighbor assignment. While intuitive, forward warping can leave unfilled holes at the destination — a limitation addressed in subsequent parts.

Observation

All five images are correctly projected onto their respective billboard regions on the canvas, demonstrating that the DLT-SVD pipeline accurately recovers the projective transformation from only four point correspondences. Minor pixel-level holes are visible at thin boundaries — an expected artifact of forward warping, where not every destination pixel may receive a mapped source pixel.

02

Marker-Based Planar Augmented Reality

This part demonstrates real-time planar AR using ArUco fiducial markers. An ArUco marker is a synthetic square marker with a unique binary pattern that can be reliably detected and its four corners precisely localized, even under varying lighting and viewpoint conditions.

For each video frame, the marker corners are detected and used as destination correspondences. The corners of the reference image serve as source correspondences. A homography is estimated between the two, and the reference image is backward-warped onto the frame: for every destination pixel within the marker region, the inverse homography H-1 maps it back to a source coordinate, which is sampled using bilinear interpolation.

Bilinear interpolation computes a weighted average of the four neighboring pixels surrounding the sub-pixel source location, producing smooth gradients and avoiding the blocky appearance of nearest-neighbor sampling. Unlike forward warping, backward warping guarantees a dense, hole-free result.

Observation

The reference image tracks the marker faithfully across all 353 frames, correctly adjusting for changes in viewpoint, scale, and orientation. The bilinear interpolation produces smooth texture rendering with no visible pixelation. Frames where the marker is partially out of view are handled gracefully — no warping is applied, and the original frame is preserved.

03

Unwarping a Hidden Secret

Two images of a QR code affixed to a wall at an angle were acquired under different conditions. The goal is to recover the flat, scannable QR code from each perspective-distorted image using backward warping with a manually-specified homography.

Observation — Same URL, Different Quality

Both recovered QR codes successfully decode to http://media.ee.ntu.edu.tw/courses/cv/25S/, yet their visual quality differs noticeably. Source A, being a digitally synthesized image, contains only projective distortion — exactly what a homography models. The recovered code is sharp and geometrically perfect.

Source B is a real camera photograph. Beyond perspective distortion, it carries radial and tangential lens distortion, sensor noise, slight motion blur, and non-uniform illumination from the environment. A single homography cannot correct these non-linear optical effects, resulting in a slightly blurred and softened QR code. The QR code standard's built-in error correction allows the code to remain scannable despite this degradation — but the recovered image is visibly lower quality.

This experiment highlights a fundamental limitation of homography: it is a purely projective model and has no capacity to compensate for the radiometric and optical imperfections inherent to physical imaging systems.

04

Panorama Stitching via RANSAC

Panorama stitching extends homography estimation to the problem of compositing multiple overlapping images into a single wide-angle view. The key insight is that when a camera rotates purely about its optical center, any two frames are related by a homography — regardless of scene depth.

Feature correspondences between consecutive frames are established automatically using ORB (Oriented FAST and Rotated BRIEF) keypoint detection and brute-force Hamming-distance descriptor matching. ORB is efficient, rotation-invariant, and performs well under illumination changes.

Because feature matches inevitably include outliers (incorrect correspondences), RANSAC (Random Sample Consensus) is used to robustly estimate the homography. In each of 2000 iterations, four correspondences are randomly sampled, a candidate homography is computed via DLT, and all matches are tested against it using a reprojection error threshold of 3.0 pixels. The homography with the most inliers is selected.

For three-image stitching, homographies are chained: the transformation mapping frame 3 into the coordinate space of frame 1 is H1→2 · H2→3. Each frame is backward-warped onto a growing canvas initialized from frame 1.

Frame 1 ↔ Frame 2 96 / 100 inliers
Frame 2 ↔ Frame 3 86 / 100 inliers
Observation — When Does Panorama Stitching Fail?

The stitched result correctly aligns all three frames into a coherent wide-angle view. A faint seam is visible at the frame 1–2 boundary, attributable to exposure differences between frames — a photometric artifact rather than a geometric one. The high inlier counts (96% and 86%) confirm that ORB + RANSAC robustly recovers the underlying homography.

However, homography-based stitching is not universally applicable. It fails in two important scenarios: (1) when the camera undergoes translation rather than pure rotation, parallax causes objects at different depths to shift by different amounts — no single homography can reconcile this; (2) when the scene is non-planar and the camera translates, different depth layers require different transformations, leading to ghosting and misalignment. Successful stitching requires either a purely rotating camera or a scene that is approximately planar.

05

Future Work

The implementations presented here demonstrate the core pipeline of homography-based image warping. Several directions could meaningfully extend this work:

Lens Distortion Correction

Pre-correcting radial and tangential lens distortion using camera calibration (Zhang's method) before applying homography would significantly improve unwarping quality for real photographs, as demonstrated in Part 3.

Multi-Band Blending

The visible seams in the panorama result from photometric differences between frames. Laplacian pyramid blending or Poisson image compositing would produce seamless transitions by blending low and high frequency components separately.

Cylindrical / Spherical Projection

For wide field-of-view panoramas spanning more than ~120°, projecting images onto a cylindrical or spherical surface before stitching reduces perspective distortion at the periphery and allows full 360° panoramas.

Deep Feature Matching

Replacing ORB with learned descriptors such as SuperPoint or DISK, combined with SuperGlue matching, would improve correspondence robustness in textureless regions and under significant illumination changes.

Optical Flow for AR Tracking

Augmenting ArUco detection with Lucas-Kanade optical flow would maintain stable AR overlay tracking in frames where the marker is partially occluded or motion-blurred, reducing flickering in the output video.

Parallax-Tolerant Stitching

Extending the pipeline with depth estimation or structure-from-motion would enable stitching of images taken with translating cameras, handling parallax through per-pixel disparity rather than a global homography.
