The Big Picture

What Problem Does NeRF Solve?

Given a handful of photos of a scene, render photo-realistic images from any new viewpoint — without a mesh, without voxels, and with only 5 MB of storage.

📷

Input

A sparse set of RGB images with known camera poses (position + orientation). For synthetic scenes: 100 views. For real scenes: 20–62 handheld photos.

🧠

Representation

A small MLP (8 layers × 256 channels) whose weights encode the entire scene as a continuous 5D function of position and viewing direction.

🎬

Output

Photo-realistic novel views from arbitrary camera positions, including specular highlights, fine-grained geometry, and translucent surfaces.

Core Insight — Instead of storing geometry as a mesh or voxel grid, encode the scene in the weights of a neural network. Query it at any continuous 3D point to get colour and density. This is compact, resolution-unlimited, and fully differentiable.
The 5D Function

What the MLP Actually Does

🔢 Inputs

Spatial position x = (x, y, z) — where in 3D space?

Viewing direction d = (θ, φ) — which direction are we looking from?

🎯 Outputs

Volume density σ — how "solid" is this point? (depends only on x)

Emitted colour c = (r, g, b) — what colour does it emit toward direction d?

FΘ : (x, d) ──→ (c, σ)
The core mapping. σ is predicted from x alone (view-independent geometry); c depends on both x and d (view-dependent appearance).
Why separate σ and c? — Volume density must be consistent regardless of viewing angle (a rock is solid from all directions). But colour can change with angle — think of a wet surface that looks bright from one angle and dark from another. This split is what enables NeRF to capture specular effects.
Volume Rendering

How a Pixel Colour is Computed

NeRF uses classic volume rendering, tracing a ray through the scene and integrating colour and density along it.

Ĉ(r) = Σᵢ Tᵢ · (1 − exp(−σᵢ δᵢ)) · cᵢ
where   Tᵢ = exp(−Σⱼ﹤ᵢ σⱼ δⱼ)
Tᵢ = accumulated transmittance (probability ray travels without hitting anything). δᵢ = distance between samples.
Key Trick #1

Positional Encoding

MLPs are biased toward low-frequency functions (Rahaman et al., 2018). Raw (x,y,z) coordinates produce blurry results. The fix: lift inputs into a high-frequency Fourier basis first.

🔇 Without Encoding

The MLP only receives raw coordinates. It struggles to represent sharp edges, fine texture, and high-frequency geometry — everything looks over-smoothed (−2.24 dB PSNR in ablation row 2).

📡 With Encoding

Each scalar coordinate p is expanded to sin(2⁰πp), cos(2⁰πp), …, sin(2^(L−1)πp), cos(2^(L−1)πp). For position: L=10 (60-D). For direction: L=4 (24-D).

γ(p) = ( sin(2⁰πp), cos(2⁰πp), sin(2¹πp), cos(2¹πp), …, sin(2^(L−1)πp), cos(2^(L−1)πp) )
Applied separately to each of the 3 position coordinates and each of the 3 direction components.
Key Trick #2

Hierarchical Sampling

Uniformly sampling 192 points per ray wastes compute on empty space. The solution: use a cheap coarse pass to find where matter is, then concentrate samples there.

🔵

Coarse Network

64 stratified samples per ray. Estimates a rough density distribution — not meant to be high quality, just informative.

📊

Build PDF

Normalise the coarse weights to get a piecewise-constant probability density function along the ray. Peaks = likely surfaces.

🎯

Fine Network

Draw 128 more samples from the PDF via inverse transform sampling. Fine network sees all 64+128 = 192 samples and produces the final colour.

Why does the coarse loss matter? — Both networks are trained simultaneously. The coarse MSE loss ensures the coarse network's density estimates are meaningful, so the PDF it produces concentrates fine samples near actual surfaces rather than empty space.
Quantitative Evaluation

Benchmark Results

Three datasets. Three metrics. NeRF leads on almost all of them — with 3000× less storage than LLFF.

Paper Review

Strengths & Weaknesses

A reviewer-style critique grounded in the paper's actual claims, tables, and appendices.

Strength S1

💡 Principled Ray Integration

Unlike SRN, which returns a single colour per ray, NeRF integrates the full density field. This produces dense, well-conditioned gradients and explains why NeRF recovers thin structures and semi-transparent surfaces that surface-based methods cannot.

Strength S2

📊 Transparent Ablation

The 9-row ablation (Table 2) cleanly isolates every design choice with exact numbers. Rare and valuable — gives reviewers and readers a causal account of what matters and by how much.

Weakness W1

⏱ 30 Seconds Per Frame

150–200M MLP queries per frame on a V100. Training: 1–2 days per scene. The paper hides this in an appendix paragraph and never compares wall-clock time against baselines — a significant omission for a "practical" view synthesis method.

Weakness W2

🔄 No Generalisation Across Scenes

A brand-new MLP is trained from scratch for every scene. LLFF trains once and runs on new scenes in under 10 minutes. The paper never tests warm-starting from a previous scene's weights or compares to generalisation-capable baselines.

Weakness W3

👁 LPIPS Regression on Real Scenes

NeRF's LPIPS on Real Forward-Facing (0.250) is worse than LLFF (0.212). The caption dismisses this with a pointer to the supplementary video — no per-scene breakdown, no analysis of when and why the perceptual quality drops.

Open Questions

Questions for the Authors

Q1

Coarse loss vs. sampling quality. The coarse network is trained with the same pixel-colour MSE as the fine network, but its purpose is to produce a good importance-sampling PDF. Could optimising for rendering quality in the coarse pass harm the quality of the PDF — e.g., by sharpening density around texture rather than geometry? Has a dedicated sampling-quality loss been considered?

Q2

NDC robustness. The NDC reparameterisation (Appendix C) assumes forward-facing captures and f→∞. How does the method behave when this assumption breaks (lateral parallax, overhead views, 360° phone captures)? No such results are reported, leaving the boundary conditions of NDC unclear.

Q3

Architecture sensitivity. The 8-layer, 256-channel MLP with a skip at layer 5 is borrowed directly from DeepSDF — a model for signed-distance regression, a structurally different task. Was any architecture search performed for NeRF's output domain (bounded density + RGB), and how sensitive are the reported numbers to depth, width, or skip placement?

Future Work

Potential Extensions

⚡

Speed: Hash Grids

Replace the MLP with a multi-resolution hash grid (Instant-NGP, 2022) to reduce per-sample cost by 100–1000×, enabling real-time rendering.

🌍

Generalisation

Condition on image-encoded latents for feed-forward reconstruction at test time — no per-scene optimisation. (pixelNeRF, IBRNet, 2021.)

🎞

Dynamic Scenes

Add a temporal coordinate or learn a canonical NeRF + deformation field from video for 4D reconstruction and novel view synthesis of moving objects.

💡

Relighting

Decompose radiance into BRDF and illumination for material editing, relighting, and physically-based rendering from casual captures.

Legacy

NeRF's Impact on the Field

Published March 2020. Within four years it spawned hundreds of papers and reshaped how the community thinks about 3D scene representation.

2020

NeRF (original)

Establishes the paradigm: continuous 5D MLP + volume rendering + positional encoding. ECCV 2020 Best Paper Honourable Mention.

2021

Generalisation wave

pixelNeRF, IBRNet, MVSNeRF add encoders for feed-forward multi-scene inference. NeRF-W handles in-the-wild, uncontrolled photo collections.

2022

Speed breakthrough

Instant-NGP (NVIDIA) reduces training to seconds with hash grids. Mip-NeRF 360 handles unbounded outdoor scenes. Thousands of NeRF variants in the literature.

2023

3D Gaussian Splatting

Replaces implicit MLP with explicit 3D Gaussians for real-time rendering — partially displacing NeRF for interactive applications, while keeping its differentiable-rendering philosophy.

2024–

Foundation models & generative NeRF

DreamFusion, Zero-1-to-3, and others combine diffusion priors with NeRF-like representations to generate 3D content from text or single images.

Applications

Where Is NeRF Used?

🎮

VR / AR Content

Capture real-world locations and reconstruct them as immersive 3D environments for headsets.

🏛

Cultural Heritage

Digitise artefacts, sculptures, and historical sites from photographs at far higher fidelity than photogrammetry.

🤖

Robotics & SLAM

Use implicit scene representations as a map for navigation and manipulation — denser and more accurate than point clouds.

🛍

E-Commerce

Reconstruct product 3D models from a few smartphone photos for interactive 360° product viewers.

🎥

Visual Effects

Reconstruct on-set geometry for compositing, relighting actors and props into new environments.

🧬

Medical Imaging

Implicit neural representations applied to novel-view synthesis from sparse CT/MRI projections, reducing radiation dose.

Why does this paper matter for your study? — NeRF is a masterclass in combining classical computer graphics (volume rendering) with modern deep learning (MLP, gradient descent, positional encoding) to solve a hard problem with surprisingly simple code. The design choices are minimal but deliberate, and the ablation study shows exactly why each one is needed. It is a blueprint for how to write a convincing methods paper.
